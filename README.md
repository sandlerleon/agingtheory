This paper proposes a unified, low-dimensional dynamical framework that models systemic biological aging as controllable information loss. By coupling an epigenetic information-loss variable, metabolic signal (glucose), active-tissue compartment (muscle reserve), and energy-storage compartment (adiposity), the model establishes three key theoretical results:

1. A Muscle-Cost Flip-Point: Demonstrates that glucose-lowering interventions that concurrently remove lean mass (such as GLP-1 receptor agonists) possess an interior optimal dose beyond which they accelerate rather than slow biological aging. Robustness testing across a 6,000-sample Monte Carlo ensemble confirms this boundary is structural and ratio-governed.
2. A Systemic-Coupling Coefficient (κ): Formalizes how tightly integrated organs propagate damage. The low limit (κ→0) mathematically reproduces the near-negligible senescence of long-lived, compartmentalized modular organisms (trees), yielding the compact relationship: systemic aging ≈ information loss × coupling.
3. The Sugar Index Muscle (SIM): Introduces a cheap, accessible composite readout (SIM = G·σ/M) derived from routine annual laboratory panels that exposes the muscle-loss flip-point where Body Mass Index (BMI) falsely reads continuous improvement.

Posed as a generalized energy-allocation compartment model rather than a species-specific physiology, this work frames its conclusions as falsifiable predictions to provide a coherent, robustness-tested scaffold for the theory and control of metabolic aging.
